# Customer Research Report: College Major & School Decision-Making

**Date**: 2026-05-27
**Method**: Digital watering hole research (Reddit, forums, competitor sites, articles)
**Status**: Initial findings — zero-to-one research, no internal assets available

---

## Executive Summary

This report synthesizes authentic customer language and pain points from students, parents, and counselors navigating the college major and school decision process. Research was conducted entirely from external sources (Reddit threads, community forums, competitor landing pages, and editorial content).

**Core finding**: The decision process is fundamentally broken across all three user groups. Students experience paralyzing uncertainty, parents are anxious and under-equipped co-decision-makers, and counselors are drowning in spreadsheet chaos. The primary tension driving every decision is **passion vs. money** — and no existing tool helps users navigate this trade-off with data rather than fear.

---

## Theme 1: "I don't know what I want to be" — Paralyzing Uncertainty

**Confidence**: HIGH
**Frequency**: Appeared across 8+ independent sources
**Intensity**: Very High — emotional language: "paralyzing," "drowning," "feel dumb," "panic"

### Summary
Students experience genuine panic about choosing a major. The pressure to "just know" creates anxiety, social comparison with peers, and second-guessing even after making decisions. The phrase "What do you want to major in?" functions as a recurring stress trigger.

### Representative Quotes
- *"When friends announce their majors, it reinforces the idea that there is something wrong with them because everyone else just seems to know. The more they get asked, 'What do you want to do?' the more panicked they feel."* — Work-Life Directions counselor blog
- *"I was a biology major but after taking some science classes and failing some of them, I changed my major to political science. But now seeing other classmates going into the medical field makes me feel dumb."* — Reddit student, 2026
- *"I don't want my life to be filled with regret."* — Same Reddit post
- *"Putting 'Undecided' is an option, but most students feel pressure to list a major."* — Work-Life Directions
- *"How can you proclaim what you're studying when you've (most likely) never taken a university-level course on the subject?"* — Michigan Daily student, 2026

### Implications for CollegeFlow
CollegeFlow should provide structured exploration frameworks, not just data dumps. The emotional dimension of major choice is under-served. Show students that "undecided" is normal, and give them tools to explore with confidence. Decision readiness scoring could reduce the "I don't know" anxiety by making progress visible.

## Theme 2: Passion vs. Money — The Core Tension

**Confidence**: HIGH
**Frequency**: Appeared across 10+ independent sources
**Intensity**: Very High — generates family arguments, resentment, guilt, and major life regrets

### Summary
Every student and parent grapples with the fundamental trade-off: follow passion or chase the paycheck. This is the #1 source of family conflict, major changes, and post-decision regret. The tension is amplified by AI anxieties about job displacement.

### Representative Quotes
- *"I still want to pursue my passion, but I also need to consider whether that field offers long-term career opportunities."* — 12th-grade student, Vietnam, 2026
- *"Sometimes I worry that if I only choose based on interest, job opportunities will be more difficult in the future, but if I choose a field just because it's trendy, I'm afraid I won't have enough interest to pursue it long-term."* — Same student
- *"AI is replacing quite a few human jobs, so I want to choose fields where AI can't yet do it."* — 12th-grade student, Hanoi-Amsterdam High School, 2026
- *"Whenever somebody asks me what I want to do in the future and I say 'I want to work at a non-profit,' they're like 'Oh that's so amazing. I wish I could do that, but I want money.'"* — University of Illinois student
- *"I want to wake up in the morning and have something that I look forward to with my job."* — Same student
- *"I want to choose fields where AI can't yet do it completely."* — Student choosing Electrical Engineering

### Implications for CollegeFlow
CollegeFlow's competitive advantage: show REAL salary data, job growth projections, AND AI-resistance scores for each major. Help students make the passion vs. money trade-off with data, not fear. This is a core differentiator from existing tools that focus only on application tracking.

## Theme 3: Parents Are Anxious, Under-Equipped Co-Decision-Makers

**Confidence**: HIGH
**Frequency**: Appeared across 10+ independent sources
**Intensity**: High — financial stress, family conflict, guilt, resentment

### Summary
Parents are deeply involved in the decision process but feel completely lost. They have zero training, face enormous financial pressure, and struggle with the tension between supporting their child vs. directing their child's future. The financial investment creates a sense of entitlement to influence the decision, which often backfires.

### Representative Quotes
- *"Most parents get zero training on how to support their kids in the high school-to-college career transition. (To be fair, parents don't get much training for any part of parenting.)"* — Work-Life Directions counselor
- *"They saved, scrimped and borrowed to give their child a head start — then watched the return on that investment evaporate."* — Reddit parent thread on degrees that "didn't pay off"
- *"A parent who takes out Parent PLUS loans at 49 is locking in monthly payments through their early 60s, the exact window they need to triple their nest egg."* — 24/7 Wall St analysis
- *"Don't make college admissions the currency of your relationship with your child!"* — College Confidential forum veteran
- *"I refused to pay for another semester after my son changed majors again."* — Reddit family dispute, 2026
- *"Loving your kid is separate from financing their college. At 49 with thin retirement savings, the most generous thing you can do is refuse to become their retirement problem."* — Financial advisor

### Implications for CollegeFlow
Parents are a distinct user group with different needs than students. They want: ROI data, cost transparency, risk assessment, and ways to support without controlling. CollegeFlow should have a "parent view" that addresses these concerns directly — financial projections, program-level graduate outcomes, and cost-of-attendance comparisons.

## Theme 4: Counselors Are Drowning in Spreadsheet Chaos

**Confidence**: HIGH
**Frequency**: Appeared across 12+ competitor sites and forums
**Intensity**: High — "drowning," "chaos," "burnout," "15 hours per week wasted"

### Summary
Independent education consultants (IECs) and school counselors manage 20-50+ students, each with 10-15 college applications = 300-750 applications. Their entire workflow lives in spreadsheets, email, and sticky notes. 80% of public school counselors report burnout.

### Representative Quotes
- *"I was drowning in spreadsheets before [platform]. Now I can see the status of all 350+ applications across 35 students in one glance. The tracker saved me at least 15 hours per week during application season."* — CollegeCounsel testimonial
- *"Managing 30+ students with 10-15 colleges each = 300+ applications. Good luck remembering everything."* — CollegeCounsel landing page
- *"I spent close to a decade as a college counsellor. Most kids I worked with had their work live in fifteen different places — three Google Docs, a Notion page, a WhatsApp thread, their mum's calendar, the inside of their head."* — Medium post by ex-counselor
- *"Burnout — 80% of public school counselors have experienced in the past 4 years."* — NEXUS software (citing EAB 2024)
- *"With 600+ students in their active ecosystem and 1,200+ served over time, tracking post-high school milestones was nearly impossible."* — Overgrad case study
- *"Most CRMs lack the workflow flexibility, automation, and customization needed for admissions counselors... 'I spend more time fighting the system than actually connecting with students.'"* — monday.com admissions page

### Implications for CollegeFlow
CollegeFlow's "Decision Workspace" directly competes with spreadsheet-based counselor workflows. The value prop must be: "replace your entire spreadsheet stack with one trusted workspace." Counselors need multi-student dashboards, deadline tracking, document management, and family communication — all in one place. The revenue share model is compelling because counselors currently get zero financial benefit from their tool spend.

## Theme 5: "My Heart Says X, My Brain Says Y" — Lifestyle vs. Prestige

**Confidence**: HIGH
**Frequency**: Appeared across 6+ independent sources
**Intensity**: High — family arguments, major changes, students lying to parents about majors

### Summary
Students making school choices face a deeply emotional conflict between campus vibe/lifestyle and career prospects/prestige. This is amplified by parent pressure. The conflict is so severe that some students lie to their parents about their major to keep tuition funding.

### Representative Quotes
- *"My heart says UGA for the lifestyle, but my brain (and my parents) say Tech for the career prospects."* — Reddit student choosing between UGA and Georgia Tech, 2026
- *"I had been lying about double-majoring in Business and Computer Science for almost a year... my mom refused every compromise."* — Reddit student, 2026
- *"He allegedly changed his major after the first semester... another major change allegedly followed shortly after. The father reportedly became frustrated."* — Reddit family dispute, 2026
- *"They saved, scrimped and borrowed... then watched the return on that investment evaporate."* — Reddit parent whose child's degree "didn't pay off"

### Implications for CollegeFlow
CollegeFlow should explicitly surface lifestyle factors (campus culture, location, social environment) alongside career data. Provide structured frameworks for having family conversations about trade-offs. The "decision readiness" product should flag when students are making choices that conflict with their stated priorities.

## Theme 6: Information Overload + Fragmentation

**Confidence**: HIGH
**Frequency**: Appeared across 10+ independent sources
**Intensity**: Medium-High — "overwhelming," "information overload," "chaos"

### Summary
Students and parents face a flood of scattered information — Common Data Sets, IPEDS, College Scorecard, US News rankings, Reddit threads, counselor advice, family opinions. No single source provides a structured, comparable view. People resort to building 13-tab Google Sheets workbooks.

### Representative Quotes
- *"Overwhelmed by the complexity and variability of admissions information"* — Reddit community analysis
- *"College info is everywhere! Websites, brochures, emails, social media – it's a flood of info for students to wade through."* — CirkledIn
- A Medium post by an ex-counselor describes building a 13-tab Google Sheets workbook: Dashboard, Master Timeline, Universities, Applications, Essays, Research, Coursework, Test Prep, Extracurriculars, Recommendations, Scholarships, Weekly Sprint, and README
- *"Start a master spreadsheet... Columns: School name, Application type, Portal, Deadline, Fee, Interview, Status, Notes."* — College Refocus guide

### Implications for CollegeFlow
CollegeFlow's core value proposition is validated — there's a clear need for a unified, authoritative data workspace. The key is combining data assets with explainable analysis (not just raw data). The fact that people build 13-tab spreadsheets shows the depth of the problem.

## Theme 7: First-Generation / Underserved Students Face Unique Barriers

**Confidence**: MEDIUM
**Frequency**: Appeared across 3+ sources
**Intensity**: High — but limited to specific demographic

### Summary
First-gen students lack mentors, family guidance, and institutional knowledge. They rely on TikTok and random internet searches for critical information about FAFSA, financial aid, and application processes.

### Representative Quotes
- *"I had to get information on the Free Application for Federal Student Aid process from TikTok."* — First-gen student from Navajo reservation
- *"I was independent doing my financial aid and I had no idea how to do my finances."* — Same student
- *"I helped start those programs because I didn't have those resources for two years of my high school career."* — Same student

### Implications for CollegeFlow
Consider a simplified, guided onboarding path for first-gen students. They need hand-holding, not data exploration. The platform should provide step-by-step guidance, not just information.

## Research Quality Assessment

### Source Distribution
| Source Type | Count | Bias Note |
|-------------|-------|-----------|
| Reddit threads | ~15 | Skews technical, opinionated, younger |
| Competitor landing pages | ~8 | Marketing language, not authentic voice |
| Community forums (College Confidential, etc.) | ~6 | Skews toward anxious/engaged parents |
| Editorial/articles | ~10 | Secondhand reporting, mixed quality |
| Ex-counselor blogs | ~4 | Professional perspective, high signal |

### Confidence Assessment
- Themes marked **HIGH** confidence appear in 3+ independent source types with consistent emotional language
- Themes marked **MEDIUM** confidence have strong signals but limited source diversity
- Recency: All sources are from 2022-2026, weighted heavily toward 2025-2026

### Known Biases
- Reddit and forum data skews toward users with strong opinions (positive or negative)
- First-gen and low-income student voices are underrepresented in the current sample
- All data is US/Western-focused; international student perspective is missing
- Competitor landing pages reflect marketing messaging, not authentic customer language

## Research Gap Analysis

### What We Still Don't Know

1. **Chinese/international student perspective**: All current data is US/Western-focused. CollegeFlow's PRD mentions a global audience. Need to research r/IntltoUSA, r/ApplyingToCollege international threads, and Chinese-language forums (知乎, 小红书) for overseas application pain points.

2. **Competitor user interviews**: No direct feedback from users of Cialfo/MaiaLearning, Naviance, Scoir, Overgrad, or CollegeCounsel. Need G2/Capterra review analysis or user interviews to understand switching triggers.

3. **Willingness to pay**: No data on what families/counselors currently pay for tools or services. Need pricing research to validate CollegeFlow's commercial model.

4. **Post-enrollment regret data**: What happens AFTER students choose? Major change rates (~30% nationally), transfer rates, dropout reasons — this validates the "decision quality" problem CollegeFlow solves.

5. **Counselor purchasing behavior**: How do IECs and school counselors evaluate and purchase software? What's the sales cycle? What features drive conversion vs. churn?

6. **Parent willingness to pay directly**: The PRD assumes parents/families are the end payers. Need to validate whether parents would pay out-of-pocket vs. expecting counselor/institution to provide.
