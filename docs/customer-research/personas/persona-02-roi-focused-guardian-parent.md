# Persona 2: The ROI-Focused Guardian — Parent

## Profile

- **Age**: 45-55 (mid-career, peak earning years)
- **Household income**: $75K-$250K+ (middle to upper-middle class; financially stretched either way)
- **Education**: College-educated themselves (often their own college experience was very different from today's landscape)
- **Geography**: Primarily US suburban; growing international segment (Chinese, Southeast Asian, Middle Eastern parents of overseas-bound students)
- **Tech literacy**: Medium — comfortable with email, web searches, basic spreadsheets. Not native to social media or modern web apps. Prefers structured, information-dense interfaces over flashy UIs.
- **Role in process**: Financial decision-maker, emotional supporter, sometimes over-director

## Primary Job to Be Done

**Functional**: Ensure that the $50K-$300K+ college investment leads to a viable career outcome. Evaluate programs, schools, and majors based on cost, graduate outcomes, and ROI — not just prestige or campus vibes.

**Emotional**: Feel like they are making a responsible financial decision while also being a supportive parent. Avoid the guilt of either pushing too hard or not doing enough. Reduce the anxiety of "will this investment pay off?"

**Social**: Be seen as a good parent who gave their child the right support without controlling their life. Avoid the Reddit horror stories of "parents paid thousands and watched the ROI evaporate."

## Trigger Events

1. **Junior year college planning begins**: Realizes they need to start budgeting for college. Panic about costs sets in.
2. **Child announces an expensive dream school**: *"My daughter's dream school costs almost $80,000 a year... she doesn't have the best grades, didn't apply for extra scholarships."* The financial reality check moment.
3. **College acceptance with financial aid letter**: Opens the award letter and realizes the gap between aid and cost. Must decide: borrow, negotiate, or redirect.
4. **Child changes major**: *"I refused to pay for another semester after my son changed majors again."* The ROI concern becomes acute when the academic path shifts.
5. **Peer comparison at dinner parties**: Other parents discuss where their kids got in, what they're studying, what it costs. Social pressure amplifies anxiety.
6. **News about degree underemployment**: Reads stories about graduates working in jobs that don't require degrees. *"They saved, scrimped and borrowed... then watched the return on that investment evaporate."*
7. **Retirement vs. college conflict**: *"I'm 49 with minimal retirement savings and want to fund my son's college: am I making a huge mistake?"* The ultimate financial tension.

## Top Pains

1. **Financial anxiety with no clarity**: No reliable way to compare the true cost and expected return of different schools and programs. *"The average undergraduate debt in the US is about $29K now... nobody should agree to debt beyond perhaps $50K tops for a BA/BS degree."*
2. **Zero training on how to help**: *"Most parents get zero training on how to support their kids in the high school-to-college career transition."* They want to be helpful but don't know how.
3. **Supporting vs. controlling tension**: Struggles to find the line between guiding their child and dictating their choices. *"Don't make college admissions the currency of your relationship with your child!"*
4. **Information asymmetry**: Child has different priorities (campus vibe, social life) while parent cares about outcomes. They speak different languages about the decision.
5. **Fear of wasted investment**: The deepest fear is paying $100K+ for a degree that leads nowhere. *"They saved, scrimped and borrowed to give their child a head start — then watched the return on that investment evaporate."*
6. **Guilt about financial limitations**: Can't afford the dream school. Feels like they're failing their child, even when it's the responsible choice.
7. **No program-level data**: University-level stats are available, but program-level graduate outcomes (salary by major, employment rate, time to employment) are opaque or inaccessible.

## Desired Outcomes

- See program-level ROI data: median salary by major, employment rates, debt-to-income ratios for each school-program combination
- Have a structured framework for family conversations about college trade-offs (cost vs. fit, prestige vs. practicality)
- Understand what "good" looks like — benchmarks for comparing schools and programs
- Feel confident that the financial decision is informed by data, not guesswork
- Support their child's exploration without being seen as controlling
- Know the realistic cost range for different paths (community college → transfer, in-state, out-of-state, private)
- Have a "parent view" of the workspace that focuses on what matters to them: cost, outcomes, risk

## Objections and Fears

- **"This is just trying to sell me something"**: Skeptical of platforms that feel like they're pushing premium upgrades. Needs to see value before paying.
- **"The data can't capture my child's uniqueness"**: Worried that algorithmic recommendations will treat their child as a number. Wants to know how the analysis accounts for individual strengths and interests.
- **"I don't trust internet advice"**: Has seen conflicting information everywhere. Needs authoritative sources (CDS, IPEDS, Department of Labor) with clear citations.
- **"My child won't listen to data anyway"**: Frustrated by the communication gap. Wants tools that help the family have productive conversations, not just more data.
- **"What if I push too hard and damage the relationship?"**: Deep fear of creating long-term resentment. *"Loving your kid is separate from financing their college."*

## Alternatives They Consider

1. **Do nothing / trust the child**: Let the kid figure it out. Least friction, highest anxiety.
2. **Google it themselves**: Search for college rankings, salary data, financial aid calculators. Fragmented, time-consuming, unreliable.
3. **Talk to other parents**: College Confidential forums, local parent groups, school PTAs. Anecdotal, emotionally charged, often misleading.
4. **Hire a college counselor**: Pay $150-$500/hr for guidance. Expensive, hard to evaluate quality, availability limited.
5. **Use the school counselor**: Free but overburdened. Caseloads of 300-500 students mean minimal individual attention.
6. **Financial aid calculators on college websites**: Scattered across dozens of sites, inconsistent formats, often inaccurate.
7. **Reddit and social media**: Ask strangers for advice. Gets passionate but conflicting opinions.

## How to Reach Them

- **Channels**: College Confidential forums, Facebook parent groups, school PTAs, email newsletters (college planning guides), search ads ("college cost calculator," "is [major] worth it")
- **Content formats**: Data-driven articles ("What Does a Psychology Degree Actually Earn You?"), cost comparison tools, financial aid explainer guides, parent webinar series
- **Influencers**: College admissions consultants, financial advisors who write about education planning, trusted parent bloggers
- **Timing**: Peak activity June-August (pre-application planning) and March-May (financial aid decision season)
- **Acquisition hook**: Free "College ROI Calculator" — input a school and major, see estimated cost vs. expected graduate earnings. Shareable result that drives word-of-mouth among parent networks.

## Ideal-of-the-Ideal

**High-value segment within this persona**: The "Data-Conscious Investor" — the parent who already thinks about college as an investment, reads financial articles about education, and wants program-level outcomes data before making decisions. They are most likely to:
- Pay for the full Decision Workspace because they see it as risk mitigation
- Use the platform to have structured family conversations (reducing conflict)
- Refer other parents in their social circle ("this tool showed us the actual salary data for every major")
- Stay engaged throughout the 4-year college journey (checking outcomes, adjusting plans)

## Who Is NOT a Good Fit

- Parents who have already decided on a specific school and are just going through the motions
- Parents who believe college choice doesn't matter at all ("any college is fine") — they won't engage with data
- Parents in extreme financial distress who cannot afford any paid tool and need free resources only
