# Persona 1: The Anxious Explorer — Student

## Profile

- **Age**: 16-18 (High school junior/senior, or college freshman exploring)
- **Academic standing**: Average to above-average GPA, but uncertain about direction
- **Family context**: Parents are involved in the decision, sometimes pushing toward specific majors. May be first-generation college applicant.
- **Geography**: Primarily US, but growing international segment (Asia, Middle East)
- **Tech literacy**: High — comfortable with TikTok, Reddit, Google Sheets, Notion. Expects modern, mobile-first interfaces.
- **College search phase**: Building initial college list → narrowing down → final applications

## Primary Job to Be Done

**Functional**: Research, compare, and ultimately decide on a college major and school that balances personal interest, career prospects, and financial reality — so they can submit applications with confidence.

**Emotional**: Feel like they are making a smart, informed decision rather than a blind guess. Reduce the anxiety of "what if I choose wrong?" and the fear of regret.

**Social**: Be able to answer "What do you want to major in?" without panicking. Show peers and parents that they have a plan. Be seen as someone who made a thoughtful choice, not a random one.

## Trigger Events

1. **Junior year college talk**: School counselor announces it's time to start thinking about college. Friends begin declaring majors. Anxiety spikes.
2. **Friend announces their major**: *"When friends announce their majors, it reinforces the idea that there is something wrong with them because everyone else just seems to know."*
3. **Failed or disliked class**: Realizes a previously assumed major isn't a good fit (e.g., failing organic chemistry, hating AP CS). Needs to pivot quickly.
4. **Parent pressure intensifies**: Parents start talking about tuition costs, ROI, and career paths. Student feels cornered.
5. **AI/job market anxiety**: Sees news about AI replacing jobs. *"AI is replacing quite a few human jobs, so I want to choose fields where AI can't yet do it."*
6. **Application deadline approaching**: Needs to finalize college list with major selections. Time pressure forces a decision.

## Top Pains

1. **Paralyzing uncertainty**: *"The more they get asked 'What do you want to do?' the more panicked they feel."* They know they need to decide but have no framework for how.
2. **Passion vs. money trap**: Torn between what excites them and what pays. *"I want to wake up in the morning and have something I look forward to"* vs. *"I wish I could do that, but I want money."*
3. **Information overload**: 15+ disconnected sources — US News, College Scorecard, IPEDS, Reddit threads, counselor advice, parent opinions, TikTok videos. *"Overwhelmed by the complexity and variability of admissions information."*
4. **Fear of irreversible mistakes**: Believes one wrong choice will ruin their life. Doesn't know that ~30% of students change majors.
5. **Comparison with peers**: Feels behind because classmates seem to have it figured out. Social pressure amplifies anxiety.
6. **No structured exploration**: Existing tools are either raw data (College Scorecard) or application trackers — nothing helps them actually explore and discover what fits.
7. **Lack of trustworthy career reality**: Can't find honest data on what people with specific majors actually do, earn, and feel 5-10 years after graduation.

## Desired Outcomes

- See a structured path from "I don't know" to "I have a shortlist I feel good about"
- Compare majors side-by-side with real data: salary, job growth, AI-resistance, daily work reality
- Understand which schools are strong for their interests, not just which are most prestigious overall
- Have a framework for having productive conversations with parents about trade-offs
- Feel confident that their choice is informed by data, not fear or peer pressure
- *"I don't want my life to be filled with regret"* — wants to minimize future regret through present diligence

## Objections and Fears

- **"This is just another app telling me what to do"**: Skeptical of recommendation engines that feel like black boxes. Needs explainable reasoning ("we recommend this because...").
- **"What if the data is wrong?"**: Trust is critical. Needs to see data provenance (CDS, IPEDS, College Scorecard) and understand limitations.
- **"I'm not smart enough for that school"**: Imposter syndrome. Needs realistic reach/target/safety categorization based on their actual profile.
- **"My parents will hate my choice"**: Fear of family conflict. Needs tools to present data-backed arguments to parents.
- **"I'll commit and then hate it"**: Fear of being locked in. Wants to see major change flexibility at each school.

## Alternatives They Consider

1. **Do nothing / go with gut**: List whatever sounds interesting, hope for the best. Most common fallback.
2. **Follow friends or trends**: Choose the same major as a friend, or chase whatever field is "hot" right now (CS, data science, pre-med).
3. **Use free spreadsheets**: Download the 13-tab Google Sheets workbook from Medium/TeachersPayTeachers. Manually research and compare schools.
4. **Ask Reddit**: Post "help me choose a major" threads. Get contradictory advice from strangers.
5. **School counselor**: If available, get generic advice from an overburdened counselor with 300+ student caseload.
6. **Paid college counselor**: Family hires an IEC ($150-$500/hr). Expensive, quality varies wildly.
7. **"Undecided"**: Apply as undecided and hope to figure it out in college. Not available at all schools.

## Key Vocabulary

| Phrase | Context |
|--------|---------|
| "I don't know what I want to do" | The default state — acknowledge and normalize it |
| "What if I choose wrong?" | The core fear — address directly with data |
| "Everyone else just seems to know" | Social comparison anxiety |
| "I want to wake up excited about my work" | Emotional job — passion |
| "But I also want money" | Practical job — financial security |
| "AI is replacing jobs" | Current anxiety about career stability |
| "I don't want my life filled with regret" | Decision quality motivation |
| "My heart says X, my brain says Y" | The lifestyle vs. prestige tension |
| "Is [major] even worth it?" | The fundamental question they're asking |

## How to Reach Them

- **Channels**: TikTok (FAFSA tips, college advice), r/ApplyingToCollege, r/college, YouTube college vlogs, Instagram college accounts, high school peer networks
- **Content formats**: Short videos (major comparisons, salary reveals), interactive quizzes ("What major fits you?"), visual comparison tools, "day in the life" content for different majors
- **Influencers**: College YouTubers, TikTok education creators, Reddit power users in r/ApplyingToCollege
- **Timing**: Peak activity August-November (application season) and March-May (decision season)
- **Acquisition hook**: Free major exploration tool that produces a personalized "Decision Readiness Score" — shareable result that drives viral loop

## Ideal-of-the-Ideal

**High-value segment within this persona**: The "Research-Obsessed Overthinker" — the student who actively reads Reddit threads, builds spreadsheets, and wants data to back their decision. They are most likely to:
- Complete onboarding fully (don't skip Step 2/3)
- Use the platform deeply (explore multiple majors, compare schools)
- Convert to paid when they see the value of structured, explainable decision support
- Refer peers ("you have to try this tool, it literally shows you the salary data for every major")

## Who Is NOT a Good Fit

- Students who have already committed to a highly specific path (e.g., pre-med at a target school) — they don't need exploration tools
- Students whose families have a single predetermined school/major with no room for input
- Students who are only looking for application deadline tracking (use a tracker, not a decision platform)
